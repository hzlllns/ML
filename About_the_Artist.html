Artist Statement
Art, like one’s sense of self, is constant work in progress.  In order to make meaning in a world where ethnicity and culture are no longer tied to one place, we must recreate meaning for ourselves in new contexts.  In the capacity of an artist and educator, I am engaged in this constant work on both the community and personal levels.


For several years now, my art has been a mode for me to explore themes such as migration, globalization and critical issues with regards to the Filipino-Canadian community.  In my art practice, the stories of my parents, the testimonies of domestic workers, the narratives of immigrant youth, and my own personal reflections become individual and collective portraits of a community in flux, grappling with the struggles brought on by migration. "What were you worried about" (2004) is a print series that explores my mother's narrative of displacement and the fear behind the belying façade of her glamorous passport photo.  Juxtaposed with the print series Papers, Please (2003), personal stories of separation, pain, and dreams give way to bureaucracy and accurately completed paperwork.  This body of work becomes a way through which others and I can empathize with the experiences that arise from displacement. 


On a more intimate level, my art explores the space between “here” and “there”, which are connected by my personal experiences, such as in Jet Lag (2005).  In this video installation, time and distance are collapsed, as my sleeping body is projected through an imagined space and into physical presence.  Memories of a childhood visit to my parents’ homeland are recounted, reflecting on the unfixed nature of identity in/formed by global migration.    


In my practice, I gather the stories of community and family members, and through processes of creation and action, help to bring these narratives to light.  Not only do I facilitate in the telling of their stories, but I also tell my own.
